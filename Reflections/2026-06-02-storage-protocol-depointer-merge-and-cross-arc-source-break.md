# Deleting `Storage.Protocol`'s raw-pointer requirement: a stronger contract, *sounder* value-backed storage, and zero-cost access — the safety trade-offs were artifacts

## What Happened

Finished the north-star `Storage.Protocol` modernization: deleted the `@unsafe func pointer(at:) -> UnsafeMutablePointer<Element>` member from the `__StorageProtocol` requirement list and from the orphan conformers, leaving a fully-typed slot contract (`capacity` + `subscript{get set}` + `initialize` + `move`) plus a `Span`/`MutableSpan`/`OutputSpan` whole-region surface. The protocol, its `Storage.\`Protocol\`` typealias, and all 7 conformances stayed; only the raw member and two orphan concrete witnesses (`Storage.Slab`/`Storage.Pool`) went.

Executed across 14 isolated cascade clones: T2 deleted the member (`storage-primitives 4aec845`, 140 tests); T3 deleted the Slab/Pool orphans and migrated ~60 test sites to the typed surface (SLI literals `slab[0]` per the principal's steer); T1 investigated the `move(at:)` SIGBUS (UNCONFIRMED — likely a stale-build false positive); T5 landed the Span-first skills; T4 ran the gates (macOS 14/14 green = 1,008 tests; SIL zero-witness 0/0 non-vacuous; termination grep clean; doc-sweep). A mid-arc `swift package update` surfaced a *different* arc's source-break (§4's `Buffer.Linear.Bounded` closure change had broken `swift-array-primitives.Array.Fixed`); the principal authorized the dev-tree fix (`d2731b1`). T6 merged all 14 repos clean into the dev-tree (disjoint divergence → zero conflicts, public URLs preserved), validated green against real resolution (tree-n 112 confirming the §4 lockstep end-to-end). ## What Worked and What Didn't

- **The refactor disproved its own predecessor's framing, empirically.** The v1.1.0 design treated safety as a *cost*: its recommended Option C was `@unsafe @_lifetime(borrow self) borrowing → MutableSpan`, coupling a public 1.0 protocol to experimental attributes and (it turned out) *still* permitting aliased mutable spans. The north-star — a plain `mutating subscript {get set}` witnessed by `_read`/`_modify` — is **sound** (exclusive `&self` forbids the aliasing Option C allowed), needs **no surface `@unsafe`**, and the SIL gate proved it **zero-cost** (the yielding witness devirtualizes to direct addressing: 0 `witness_method`/0 `class_method`, 16 direct-addressing instructions, correct runtime). Every leg of the assumed safety-vs-capability-vs-performance trade-off fell.
- **The biggest correctness payoff was `Storage.Inline` (value-backed).** Under Option C, Inline got a *silently unsound* witness — a `MutableSpan` formed over `self`'s own `@_rawLayout` value from `borrowing self`, an exclusivity violation laundered by `_overrideLifetime`. Under the north-star, `_modify` takes exclusive `&self`, so the hazard *cannot arise*. The de-pointering didn't just remove a pointer; it **closed a soundness hole** the raw approach had — the typed surface is *strictly more sound* than the raw pointer for value-backed storage, not merely equivalent.
- **The deletion was provably safe** because every residual `.pointer(at:` has a *concrete* receiver — the termination grep's real job was proving zero `some Storage.Protocol`.pointer(at:) calls, not zero pointer calls.
- **What needed real judgment: the retention boundary.** Not every pointer could go; the work was distinguishing a *genuine language limitation* (Heap/Inline) from *incomplete migration* (Pool/Slab — deleted). A short process-friction note: during the (now CI-deferred) Linux gate I spawned redundant wait-loops and double-backgrounded a launch, which cascaded into a wedged Docker VM — the [REFL-017] anti-pattern.

## Patterns and Root Causes

**1. "Safety vs capability vs performance" was assumed to be a trilemma; it was three artifacts.** The predecessor doc recorded the north-star as an "honest ceiling" sidebar precisely because it assumed reaching it cost something — that *safe-looking* required `@unsafe`+experimental `@_lifetime`, that the uninitialised-tail *required* a raw `UnsafeMutableBufferPointer` ("`MutableSpan` is UB over uninitialised memory"), and that a typed witness would cost dispatch. Each was an artifact, not a law: the uninit-tail claim **predated `OutputSpan` (SE-0527)**, which is *exactly* the safe append-into-uninit primitive; the soundness was reachable by an **unexplored construction** (yielding `_modify` over exclusive `&self`, not a returning span over a shared borrow); the dispatch cost was **empirically zero** (SIL). The meta-lesson is durable and transferable: *when a design frames safety as a trade-off against capability or performance, suspect the framing first* — the trade-off is usually an outdated-primitive-set artifact (pre-`OutputSpan`) or an unexplored sound construction, and the "ceiling" sidebar is often the actual answer demoted by the assumption.

**2. Deleting a protocol's most-powerful requirement *strengthens* the contract, and the deletion-safety axis is generic-vs-concrete.** A raw `pointer(at:)` *requirement* is a capability leak: the protocol obliges all 7 conformers to vend a raw mutable pointer and lets any generic consumer do arbitrary arithmetic and aliasing — the protocol *promising unsafety*. Replacing it with `subscript`/`initialize`/`move` narrows the promise to exactly the slot operations that exist; the contract gets *tighter*, not weaker. Deletion is safe **iff no generic-over-the-protocol code uses the member** — concrete uses survive on the conformer's concrete method, which is why removing the *requirement* (T2) and removing the *concrete orphans* (T3) are separable operations with *different* verifications (no generic caller vs no caller at all). Presence-vs-absence is the wrong grep; generic-vs-concrete-receiver is the right one.

**3. A sanctioned `@unsafe` escape hatch is a *typed boundary around exactly what the language cannot yet express* — with a citation and a REMOVE-WHEN; otherwise it is debt.** Heap/Inline retain `pointer(at:)` for three *specific, cited* reasons, each a real language limitation: no in-place mutable binding to a `~Copyable` enum payload (`cannot partially reinitialize 'self'`) for `Small._modify`; a `~Escapable` span can't live in an `Escapable` `Walk` iterator; and the Property.View move route (the unconfirmed T1 SIGBUS). Pool/Slab had *no* such limitation → deleted. That is the line between a sanctioned hatch and migration debt: the hatch must name the limitation and the condition under which it dissolves. A corollary surfaced in the tests — the Slab/Pool CoW tests had been coupled to the *access mechanism* (`pointer(at:)`) when they meant to assert the *type's* shared-backing semantics; removing the hatch forced them onto the typed surface (verified-identical CoW), and the rewrite was a clarification, not a loss.

Both are real but smaller than the above.)*
