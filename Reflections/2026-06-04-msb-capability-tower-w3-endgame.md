# MSB capability-tower W3 endgame: the bd04f32 deinit wall makes cleanup oracles irreducibly class-bound; canonical-basename worktrees, mirror-first HOLD designs, and pin-assert are the only honest local clean-rooms

> Scope note: this is the closeout reflection for a multi-session program (W0 spike → W4 bit-axis → W1 capability lift → W2 reconform → W3 re-layering → merge/publish/push). It banks the *durable* findings the supervisor flagged for follow-up #17, drawn first-hand from `msb-tower-PROGRESS.md`, `msb-w3-buffer-s-fanout-report.md`, and `msb-w3-review-9-consolidated.md` §13. It is not a re-narration of every wave; the per-package merge-review lives in the #9 doc and the staged record in PROGRESS.

## What Happened

The MSB program lifted three cross-cutting capabilities — `Span.Protocol` / `Span.Borrowed.Protocol` / `Span.Mutable.Protocol` (new `swift-span-primitives`) and `Store.Protocol` (the four element-store ops, new `swift-store-primitives`) — out of the Memory/Storage namespaces, then reconformed the whole MSB tower onto the neutral capabilities so that no `Memory.Contiguous.Protocol` / `Memory.Contiguous.Borrowed` precomposed protocols survive and the `memory→byte` namespace edge is severed entirely. W3 was the endgame: the tower re-layering (`Storage.Split<Lanes, Elements>` / `Storage.Flat<Substrate>` element-store composition; the `Buffer<S: Storage.Protocol>` substrate-reparam of every dense discipline; the `Buffer.Aligned`/`Unbounded` → `Memory.Aligned`/`Unbounded` re-domain; the `Growth.Policy` hoist out of Buffer to kill an upward dep), the `.Borrowed` 6→2 prune, then the full merge/publish/push closeout under a six-stage principal authorization (D-1…D-6).

The endgame landed. The push (Stage 6) put 53 repos on `origin/main`, including 5 first-content pushes (span, store, growth, memory-aligned, memory-unbounded) and 3 archives (storage-slab, buffer-aligned, buffer-unbounded), 0 failures, all repos verified `HEAD == origin/main`, visibility unchanged (all private; the public flip remains a separate gate). The 0-witness property — the program's reason for existing, that generic substrate composition vends NO `witness_method` in `-O` consumer SIL — was preserved end-to-end and receipt-verified on the real production modules (`/tmp/msb-real-tower-receipt`, 0 `witness_method` in 529-line `-O` SIL).

The path there was a sequence of compiler walls, each of which taught something durable. This reflection banks them.

### The findings banked (the supervisor's #17 content spec)

**1. The bd04f32 compiler wall — conditionally-Copyable generic structs cannot declare `deinit`.** When the `Buffer.Slab<S>` full-arity reparam was attempted, the compiler rejected it: `deinitializer cannot be declared in generic struct that conforms to Copyable`. A type that is `Copyable` only conditionally (`Copyable where S: Copyable`) still must satisfy the Copyable layout in the unconstrained case, and Copyable structs cannot carry a `deinit`. But the bitmap-driven auto-cleanup of a slab (walk occupancy, deinitialize occupied slots on drop) IS a `deinit`. The two requirements are mutually exclusive in today's Swift. The consequence is structural, not cosmetic: **any cleanup oracle on a conditionally-Copyable discipline requires a class boundary.** This grounded the whole `①-rev=(i)` decision (KEEP `Storage.{Arena, Pool, Slab}` — they are irreducible) and the Box-relocation family (slab pilot `c3d3bb5`: a private `final class Box` holds storage + bitmap + `deinit`, the struct holds only the box, CoW via `isKnownUniquelyReferenced`). The pilot's "DOES NOT BUILD" record was committed deliberately at `bd04f32` as the wall evidence. The same wall bit one level down in ASK-1 (a conditionally-Copyable `Buffer.Ring` cannot self-clean its slots, so it must delegate cleanup to the storage's deinit — which requires the storage to be Heap-concrete, contradicting the generic-substrate field) and again in ASK-6 (arena/linked, below). The general law that emerged and was supervisor-affirmed: **exactly one cleanup-truth-holder per discipline.** Arena's Box correctly has NO deinit because the held `Storage.Arena` IS the surviving oracle (a Box deinit would duplicate the cleanup truth, violating the "no NEW duplication" gate); Slab's Box DOES have a deinit because its oracle was deleted and absorbed. Whether the truth-holder is the Box or the held concrete substrate is the per-discipline judgment.

**2. Canonical-basename worktree identity mechanics.** A path-dep's SwiftPM package identity is its *directory basename*, and a root path-dep override beats a transitive url-dep ONLY when the basenames match. The original `--w2`-suffixed worktrees (`swift-byte-primitives--w2`) produce a DISTINCT identity that does NOT unify with a transitive mirror-resolved url-dep to the canonical name (`swift-byte-primitives`, pulled via `memory→bit-index→byte`) → two checkouts, identical target names → `multiple similar targets`. The fix was to re-home every changed worktree to its canonical basename in a separate parent dir (`/Users/coen/Developer/.msb-wt/swift-<name>`) on the same feature branch, so the root path-dep's identity matches the transitive url's identity and SwiftPM's local-override unifies them — no `--w2` suffix, no global mirror mutation. Chasing the `--w2` mesh package-by-package never converged (each consumer revealed a new transitive `--w2` need: binary→byte, consumers→buffer-linear, set-ordered→hash-table→buffer-slots); the canonical-basename whole-closure integration done once at closeout was the only convergent path. Stale post-move `.build` ModuleCaches bake absolute paths and require `rm -rf .build` per package after a `git worktree move`.

**3. F1 — mirror-first HOLD designs (Stage 2 halt).** The merge brief's plan was to merge worktree branches to main carrying `path:` deps on the 5 unpublished packages (span/store/growth/memory-aligned/memory-unbounded) as a temporary HOLD, reverting to url post-publish. SwiftPM forbids it: **a package resolved by url/revision cannot carry `path:` deps** (verbatim error banked). HOLD-style path-deps are structurally unsound for url-consumed packages. The resolution inverted the order: create the 5 repos (private, empty) + add mirror entries FIRST, then flip all HOLD lines to url. A mirror entry makes a url resolve to the local checkout WITHOUT the remote needing any content — so the urls resolve locally against empty repos, and the package content is whatever the local clone holds. This is the mirror-first publication primitive: the mirror, not the remote, is what makes a url-form dep resolvable pre-publish.

**4. F2 — the stale shared cache + pin-assert (Stage 2 halt, second cause).** A warm `swift build` was serving a *no-branch commit* (`11e1611`) out of `~/Library/Caches/org.swift.swiftpm` — the SHARED swiftpm cache, which `rm -rf .build` does NOT touch. `rm -rf .build` is NOT a cold build; the package-cache survives it and can serve commits that exist on no branch. The only honest local clean-room is a PIN-ASSERT: for every package, clear BOTH `.build` AND `Package.resolved`, then after resolve assert that every `Package.resolved` revision equals its dep's live `main` HEAD. Anything short of that can pass against phantom cached state. The cold re-run with pin-assert (36/36) is what made the Stage-2 greens trustworthy.

**5. [HANDOFF-040/041] — two distinct regex/gate-grep lessons.** (a) [HANDOFF-041]: a gate-grep's *width* must match the criterion's text. The G5 old-form-residual gate's criterion was "code AND docs/comments," but the command used `--include="*.swift"` over `Sources/` — narrower than its own criterion. It reported EMPTY while six real residuals hid in `.docc` examples and a heap *live-code* site (masked because heap's test target doesn't build under the external exception). (b) [HANDOFF-040]: a pattern's character-class must cover the value domain. The G5 inner class lacked `0-9`, so every digit-bearing spelling (`Tree<Int>.N<2>.Node`, `UInt8`) was invisible to every prior sweep until a digit-inclusive re-sweep caught them. Both are the same meta-failure (the gate under-matched its intent) with two different mechanisms (scope vs character-class). The standing rule that emerged: width-check every gate-grep against its criterion text BEFORE declaring it empty.

**6. The push-set predicate lesson (PERMANENT).** Push-set membership is SET-MEMBERSHIP against an enumerated window, never `ahead>0`. Stage 6 over-included 7 repos (effect/graph/index/machine/range/reference/set) — each had exactly one principal-confirmed `Remove Skills/SKILL.md` commit, so each was "ahead," but none was in the W3-enumerated push window. The content was principal-cleared and the over-inclusion was honestly disclosed, but the predicate was wrong: "has unpushed commits" is not "is in the set I was authorized to push." This is now a permanent process lesson per the supervisor.

**7. The suppression-restatement family (Findings 1/11).** A bare `extension X: Y {}` or `extension X {}` on a type with a *suppressed* (`~Copyable`) generic parameter silently re-imposes `Copyable` on that parameter. This bit at every layer: protocol refinement (`protocol Span.Mutable.Protocol: Span.Borrowed.Protocol` must restate `, ~Copyable`; same for `__StorageProtocol: __StoreProtocol`); multi-param generic extensions (`Storage.Split` — restating `~Copyable` on only `Element` implicitly Copyable-constrains `Lanes`/`Elements`); conformances (the ASK-5 `extension Storage.Heap: Span.Protocol {}` re-pinning the span property to require Copyable elements; the `Array.Fixed: Collection.Access.Random {}` case the #9 review OVERTURNED — a bare conformance silently Copyable-pinning the capability's *existence* for move-only elements). The standard failure mode is that **Copyable-element tests MASK it** — the defect only surfaces with move-only-element coverage. Two sub-rules crystallized: restate `~Copyable` on EVERY suppressed param everywhere; and Finding 11 gates WHEN a conformance exists, not the witness shapes inside it (so "it's a marker protocol with no witnesses" is not a valid reason to skip the restatement). A critical asymmetry to bank: `S.Element` *projection* constraints are LEGAL on extensions but ILLEGAL on suppressible-protocol (`Copyable`/`Sendable`) conformances (`conditional conformance to suppressible protocol 'Copyable' cannot depend on 'S.Element: Copyable'`).

**8. Swift.Span / Storage namespace shadowing (Finding 2).** Once `Span_Protocol_Primitives` is imported, the `Span` enum shadows `Swift.Span` — every `Swift.Span<…>` in a witness signature, iterator return type, or body must be module-qualified or it resolves to the namespace enum and fails. The Wheel nested-`Storage`-type case (Stage 5) is the sibling: a namespace enum named `Storage` shadows both the stdlib type and a sibling `Storage` type; qualify via the module. This recurred often enough across cores, prune, and the consumer wave to be a reflexive check, not a surprise.

### Worth one line each (the lift-seam / form-α / ledger / receipt findings)

- **The (b′) lift seam.** ASK-1's resolution was to lift ONLY `initialization` onto `Storage.Protocol` (the converged "uninit↔init transition" concern, NOT the full Heap surface) and make span-dependent conformances conditional `where S: Span.Protocol`, rewriting `span(range:)` → native `span.extracting(range)`. This cleared the entire conformance-layer wall while keeping `Storage.Protocol` minimal. The rejected (b)-as-asked (put the whole `span` family on `Storage.Protocol`) would have violated the capability normal form; (c) would have reopened bd04f32. The seam is: lift the *minimal* shared concern, gate the rest conditionally.
- **Form-α: MutableSpan is never the generic mutate seam — and this is borrow-checker-structural, not stylistic.** A generic `mutableSpan` *property* walls even with `@_lifetime(&self)` on the requirement (an opaque-generic getter result is a borrow; forwarding it overlaps `&self`). The only viable form is a Heap-pinned `<E> where S == Storage<E>.Heap` *method* (form α) — a computed property cannot carry the same-type pin, only a method can. So mutable-span access on a generic substrate is necessarily a method, not a property.
- **The two-bucket / three-bucket exception ledger discipline.** Blocked-but-correct packages were sorted into named buckets, not lumped: (bucket 1, external-arc) heap/stack — wait on the collection↔iterator drift, may legitimately be red at push; (bucket 2, in-program-deferred) set-ordered — green is deterministic at the #13 wave; (bucket 3) async — accepted-red pre-existing test failures routed to a follow-up. The discipline is that "blocked" is not one status — each blocker gets a named cause class and an unblock event, and only a genuinely NEW cause class stops the program.
- **The honest-record receipt form (superseded-kept-visible).** The whole-closure re-verify receipt keeps superseded lines VISIBLE with corrections appended, rather than rewriting to the final state. This is now the standard for every gate artifact in the program: raw run preserved, supersessions visible, corrections appended. It makes a receipt auditable — a reader can see what was first claimed, what overturned it, and why.

## What Worked and What Didn't

**Worked:**
- **Committing the walls as evidence.** Every compiler wall (`bd04f32`, ASK-2's recursive-same-type rejection, ASK-6's four-cell exclusion table) was committed as a "DOES NOT BUILD — do not merge" record, not discarded. This turned each dead-end into a citable artifact that grounded a principal decision (`①-rev`, the ⑤ namespace-reparam choice). The walls ARE the design rationale.
- **Probe-before-fan-out.** The `/tmp/msb-buffer-s-probe` validated the language risks (conditional Copyable on a namespace `~Copyable` param, `@_rawLayout(likeArrayOf: S.Element)`, the Box-relocated oracle) and the 0-witness property before the production migration. But its FIDELITY GAP (it self-tracked `count` and used only `Store.Protocol` ops, never exercising `initialization`-tracking or span-based iteration) is exactly why ASK-1 wasn't caught at probe time — a reduced probe clears the risks it models and is silent on the ones it doesn't.
- **Per-package STOP on a new wall class.** The fan-out agents correctly STOPped on ASK-1/2/4/5/6 rather than power-through with workarounds, surfacing each as a class-(c)/API-shape decision. The standing pre-authorization (Finding-1/11 restatement, ArrayLiteral-drop, form-α, (ii′)) let repeat-class fixes proceed without re-asking while genuinely new walls stopped — the right granularity.
- **Pin-assert and mirror-first as recovery, not prevention.** Both Stage-2 halts (F1, F2) were caught by the verification discipline (pin-assert exposed the stale cache; the url-resolution error exposed the HOLD-path unsoundness), not anticipated. The cold-clean-room machinery earned its place by catching real phantom-green.

**Didn't:**
- **The probe fidelity gap cost a full fan-out STOP.** ASK-1 was a wall the probe COULD have modeled (the dense disciplines' Heap-concrete `initialization`/`span` dependence was knowable from W2) but didn't, because the probe's `Ring` was reduced. The fan-out was dispatched, hit the wall, and STOPped — a round-trip that a higher-fidelity probe (one that exercised the real conformance set) would have avoided.
- **A `git add -A` slip mislabeled two commits.** `dd3d2aa`/`6f6744c` ("Prose-only") additionally swept in pre-existing `--w2`→canonical manifest normalizations; the commit messages are inaccurate for those two. Left standing per no-history-rewriting; explicit-path-add reaffirmed. The recurrence of this exact class (it is in MEMORY.md feedback) suggests the explicit-add discipline needs a mechanical pre-commit guard, not just vigilance.
- **Two narrow-gate misses on the SAME gate.** G5 missed residuals twice (scope width, then character-class) before the canonical form. A gate that fires EMPTY on a maturing criterion deserves suspicion proportional to the criterion's complexity — both misses were "the gate under-matched its own intent," which a width-check-against-criterion-text step catches mechanically.

## Patterns and Root Causes

**The deepest pattern: conditional-Copyable + cleanup is a structural triangle, and the language picks two.** bd04f32, ASK-1, and ASK-6 are not three bugs — they are one constraint seen from three angles. A discipline wants (a) a generic substrate field (`var storage: S`), (b) conditional Copyable (`Copyable where S: Copyable`, so move-only and value-semantic elements both work), and (c) automatic cleanup of occupied slots on drop. Swift lets you have any two:
- (a)+(b) but not (c): the struct can't carry a `deinit` (Copyable layout forbids it) — the bd04f32 wall.
- (a)+(c) but not (b): make it unconditionally `~Copyable` so it can carry a `deinit` — loses the Copyable consumers.
- (b)+(c) but not (a): pin the field to a Heap-concrete type whose own `deinit` cleans up — loses the generic substrate (the ASK-1 form).

The Box-relocation pattern is the escape: move the (a)+(c) pair into a `final class Box` (classes carry deinits freely and are reference types, so the struct's Copyable layout is just a pointer), leaving the struct with (b) trivially. The "exactly one cleanup-truth-holder per discipline" rule is the invariant that keeps Box-relocation honest: you put the deinit EITHER on the Box OR on a held concrete substrate, never both, because two deinits walking the same occupancy is a double-free waiting to happen. This is why arena's Box has no deinit (the held `Storage.Arena` is the truth-holder) and slab's does (its oracle was absorbed). The general lesson for the ecosystem: **when a generic value-type discipline needs lifecycle management, the lifecycle lives at a class boundary, and the type system will force you to find where.**

**The second pattern: "clean" is a claim about a tool's reach, not about reality.** F2 (stale cache), the G5 narrow-grep misses, and the probe fidelity gap are the same epistemic failure: a verification tool reported "clean/empty/passing" while reality differed, because the tool's REACH was narrower than its apparent claim. `rm -rf .build` claims "cold" but doesn't reach the shared cache. `grep --include="*.swift"` claims "no residuals" but doesn't reach `.docc`. A reduced probe claims "the shape works" but doesn't reach the real conformance set. The fix in every case is to align the tool's reach with the claim's scope: pin-assert (reach the resolved revisions, not just the build dir), width-check the grep against its criterion, exercise the real surface in the probe. This generalizes [REFL-011] (primary-source re-derivation) from *state claims* to *verification-tool claims*: a green from a tool is only as trustworthy as the tool's reach, and the reach must be checked against what the green is being read to mean.

**The third pattern: suppression annotations don't propagate, and tests don't reveal it.** Findings 1 and 11 recurred at least six times across the program because `~Copyable` suppression is local — every extension, refinement, and conformance must restate it, and the compiler silently re-imposes `Copyable` where you don't. The reason it kept slipping through is the masking: a Copyable-element test suite exercises the Copyable instantiation, where the (wrongly) re-imposed constraint is satisfied, so everything passes. The defect is only visible with move-only-element coverage. This connects to a standing ecosystem discipline (`feedback_extension_implies_copyable.md`, `feedback_correctness_and_evergreen.md`): `~Copyable` adoption is judged on structural correctness, not consumer demand — and structural correctness here MEANS restating suppression everywhere AND covering move-only elements in tests, because the test gap is what lets the structural defect ship. The `Array.Fixed` overturn in the #9 review is the canonical instance: a reviewer's "marker protocol, no witnesses, fine" reasoning missed that the bare conformance itself pins the capability — the existence of the conformance, not its contents, is the gate.

**The fourth pattern: package identity is a filesystem fact, and the resolver composes it transitively.** The canonical-basename mechanics (Finding 2), the `--w2` collision class, and the mirror-first HOLD resolution (F1) are all consequences of one rule: SwiftPM identity = directory basename, and a local override only wins when its identity matches the transitively-resolved identity. The `--w2` suffix broke this by minting a distinct identity that couldn't unify with the canonical transitive url. The lesson — already partially in MEMORY.md (`reference_swiftpm_mirror_identity_basename.md`) — is that any local-override scheme for a multi-package closure must preserve canonical basenames, because the resolver unifies on basename, not on "this is obviously the same package." Mirror-first publication is the same rule on the publish axis: a mirror entry makes a url resolve to a local basename, decoupling "url-form dep" from "remote exists."
